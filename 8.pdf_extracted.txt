Chapter 8 Meaning and Context
I. What is context?
As most words have more than one meaning, it is often impossible to tell the meaning of a
word until it is used in discourse, either spoken or written. That is where the context comes in.
No matter how many meanings a word may have, there will be no risk of misunderstanding,
when it occurs in a particular context, for meaning lives in context and the context throws light
on meaning.
II. Types of Contexts:
1. Linguistic Context:
In a narrow sense, context refers to words, clauses, sentences in which a word appears, and it
can also extend to a paragraph, a whole chapter or even the entire book. It is called linguistic
context.
Linguistic context can be further divided into lexical context and grammatical context.
2. Extra-linguistic Context:
In a broad sense, context refers to the physical situation where a word is employed. It is called
extra-linguistic context (non-linguistic context), which includes:
1) Situational Context (the actual speech situation):
 the participants (addresser and addressee)
 time and place
2) Context of Setting (the socio-cultural context, etc.)
e.g., Cultural Context: the different image of “dog” activated in English culture and Chinese
culture
III. Functions of Context:
1) Eliminating Ambiguity
Ambiguity often arises due to polysemy and homonymy when the word is used in inadequate
context.
The full context can narrow down the meaning of a word or structure. With the context
extended, linguistic or sometimes extra-linguistic, no ambiguity or misunderstanding would arise.
2) Indication of Referents 指示所指对象
A large number of deictic words such as now/then, here/there, this/that, are often used to
refer directly to the personal, temporal or locational characteristics of the situation.
Without clear context, the reference can be very confusing.
