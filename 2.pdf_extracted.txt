1. Introduction
1.1 Classification of Languages
1.1.1 Typological Classification（类型分类法）：
1） 根据词汇的形态特征进行分类：
可分为四类：孤立语（isolating language词根语），粘着语（agglutinating language），屈折语
（inflecting language），多式综合语（incorporating language）；粘着语和屈折语又可统称为综
合语（synthetic language）。
孤立语：一般说来，每个词只含一个语素；其特点不是通过词的内部形态变化来表达语法作
用，而是通过虚词和词序来表达。越南语被认为是最接近孤立语的一种语言，彝语、壮语、
苗语也属于孤立语的范畴。
粘着语和屈折语（综合语）：一个词通常由一个以上的语素构成；其特点是有词的形态变化，
语法关系通过词的形态变化来表示。两者间的区别在于语素之间的结合方式不同：在粘着语
中，语素间的界线非常分明；而在屈折语中，却没有分明的界线。大部分的欧洲语言属于综
合语。
多式综合语：是一种更高程度的综合语，许多美洲原住先民的语言属于多式综合语，如印第
安语。
尽管汉语中有一些属于综合语的形态成分，但一般认为汉语更接近于孤立语。与欧洲其
他许多语言相比，英语的词汇形态也偏向孤立语型，但与汉语相比，却更偏向于综合语型，
因为综合语的特点是词缀丰富。从派生词和复合词所占比重来看，派生词在英语中的比例远
高于汉语，而复合词的比例在汉语中高于英语。
2）根据语言的句法特征进行分类：
这种分类方式把语言分为：综合语和分析语
Synthetic language（综合语）：shows the relation of words by inflection；日语、俄语、德语、
印地语属于综合语（包括屈折语、粘着语和多式综合语）。
Analytic language（分析语）：indicates the relation of words in a sentence by word order；汉语、
英语、法语、保加利亚语属于分析语（仅包括孤立语）。
1.1.2 Genetic Classification（谱系分类法）
根据语言的共同来源和亲属关系可将分为：Sino-Tibetan（汉藏语系），Indo-European（印
欧语系），Semito-Hamitic（闪含语系），Bantu（班图语系），Uralic（乌拉尔语语系），Altaic
（阿尔泰语系），Malayo-Polynesian（马来-波利尼西亚语系），Indian（印第安语系）， Caucasian
Family（高加索语系），Austronesian family（南岛语系），Austro-Asiatic Family（南亚语系）。

1.2 The Indo-European Language Family
It is assumed that the world has nearly 3,000 languages, which can be grouped into roughly
300 language families on the basis of similarities in their basic word stock and grammar. Chinese
is a member in the Sino-Tibetan Family, while English is a member in the Indo-European Family.
The Indo-European is made up of most of the languages of Europe, the near East, and India.
Linguists have divided the Indo-European languages into several groups（语族）, and then
subdivided the different groups into branches（语支）.
The Indo-European languages accordingly fall into an Eastern set: Balto-Slavic（波罗的—斯
拉夫语族）, Indo-Iranian（印度—伊朗语族）, Armenian and Albania; a Western set: Celtic, Italic,
Germanic, Hellenic（古希腊语族）, Hittite and Tocharian.
In the Eastern set, Armenian and Albania are the sole modern language in the two respective
families. The Balto-Slavic comprises such modern languages as Russian, Prussian, Lithuanian,
Polish, Czech, Bulgarian and Slovenian. In the Indo-Iranian are found Persian, Bengali, Hindi,
Romany.
In the Western set, Hittie and Tocharian find no descendants of their own and Greek is the
modern language derived from Hellenic. In the Celtic, we find Scottish, Irish, Welsh, Breton,
Cornish and Pictish. The Latin language and the five Romance languages, namely, Portuguese,
Spanish, French, Italian and Roumanian, all belong to the Italic. Finally comes the Germanic
family, which is our chief concern as English and its nearest relations are all members of this
family. First, we have the four Northern Germanic: Norwegian, Icelandic, Danish and Swedish,
which are generally known as Scandinavian languages. Then come the four Western Germanic,
German, Dutch, Flemish and English.

2. A Historical Overview of the English Vocabulary
2.1 Old English Period (450-1066): also called the Anglo-Saxon Period
2.1.1 Historical Development
The First Inhabitants: Celts, also called Britons are believed to be the first people who inhabited
the land that was later to become England; Their language — Celtic is still another branch of the
Indo-European language family.
The Roman Invasion: In 55—54 B.C. the Romans led by Julius Caesar the Great invaded the
British Isles, and then occupied the land from A.D. 43 until 410. The Latin of the Roman Legions
became the second major language known in England.
The Invasion of Anglo-Saxons: The withdrawal of Roman troops invited the invasion by the
Picts and Scots from the North. The Celts appealed to Germanic warriors for assistance in
defending their land. Soon the Germanic tribes, called Angles, Saxons and Jutes poured in great
numbers. The new allies of the Celts became their conquerors, and took permanent control of
England. Their language, historically known as Anglo-Saxon, dominated and almost totally
blotted out the Celtic. It is at this time (about 450 AD) when the English language began.
2.1.2 Two events in the Old English Period
1) The introduction of Christianity: In the 6th century, Latin-speaking Roman missionariescame
to spread Christianity in Britain. The introduction of Christianity had a great impact on the English
vocabulary.
It brought many new ideas and customs and also many religious terms from Latin.
2) The Northman Invasion (the 9th century): the land was invaded again by Norwegian and
Danish Vikings. They came first to plunder, then to conquer. With the invaders, many
Scandinavian words came into English.
2.1.3 Characteristics of Old English
• In Old English period, there is a frequent use of coinages known as ‘kennings’, which
refers to vivid figurative descriptions often involving compounds.
• The absence of a wide-ranging vocabulary of loanwords force people to rely more on
word-formation processes based on native elements.
• Old English was a highly inflected language just like modern German. Nouns, pronouns,
adjectives, verbs and adverbs have complex endings or vowel changes, or both.
• Old English is believed to contain about 24,000 different lexical items. However, old
English differs greatly from the language we use today.

2.2 The Middle English Period (1066-1500):
2.2.1 The Norman Conquest:
 The Normans invaded England from France in 1066.
The Norman French imposed a new political and cultural life on the land of Anglo-Saxon,
and changed greatly the development of the English language. The Norman Conquest virtually
introduced French-English bilingualism into England. The situation of the simultaneous existence
of three languages (French-English-Latin) continued for over a century.
 The Old English began to undergo much change when the Norman Conquest started a
continual flow of French words into English. The Norman dialect of French became the language
of the upper class, while English completely lost its scholarly and literary importance.
 By the end of the 11th century, almost all of the people who held political or social power
and many of those in powerful church positions were of Norman French origin. This resulted in a
massive borrowing of French words into English vocabulary.
 By the end of the 13th century, English gradually came back into the schools, the law courts,
and government and regained social status thanks to Wycliffe translation of the Bible and the
writings of Chaucer.
2.2.2 Characteristics of Middle English
 The development was the steady erosion of the Old English inflectional systems. Middle
English retained much fewer inflections, with a standard word order making up for their loss. If
we say old English was a language of full endings, Middle English was one of leveled endings.
 A lot of French words poured into English, covering every aspect of culture and society.
 Latin words continued to make their way into the English language.

Difference between American English and British English:
While there are certainly many more varieties of English, American English and British
English are the two varieties that are taught in most ESL/EFL programs. Generally, it is agreed
that no one version is "correct" however, there are certainly preferences in use. The three major
differences between American and British English are:
 Pronunciation - differences in both vowel and consonants, as well as stress and intonation
 Vocabulary - differences in nouns and verbs, especially phrasal verb usage
 Spelling - differences are generally found in certain prefix and suffix forms
The most important rule of thumb is to try to be consistent in your usage. If you decide that
you want to use American English spellings then be consistent in your spelling (i.e. The color of
the orange is also its flavour - color is American spelling and flavour is British), this is of course
not always easy - or possible. The following guide is meant to point out the principal differences
between these two varieties of English.
a. Use of the Present Perfect
In British English the present perfect is used to express an action that has occurred in the
recent past that has an effect on the present moment. For example:
I've lost my key. Can you help me look for it?
In American English the following is also possible:
I lost my key. Can you help me look for it?
In British English the above would be considered incorrect. However, both forms are
generally accepted in standard American English. Other differences involving the use of the
present perfect in British English and simple past in American English include already, just and
yet.
British English:
I've just had lunch I've already seen that film Have you finished your homework yet?

b. Possession
There are two forms to express possession in English. Have or Have got
Do you have a car? Have you got a car?
He hasn't got any friends. He doesn't have any friends.
She has a beautiful new home. She's got a beautiful new home.
While both forms are correct (and accepted in both British and American English), have got (have
you got, he hasn't got, etc.) is generally the preferred form in British English while most speakers
of American English employ the have (do you have, he doesn't have etc.)
c. The Verb Get
The past participle of the verb get is gotten in American English. Example He's gotten much better
at playing tennis. British English - He's got much better at playing tennis.
d. Vocabulary
Probably the major differences between British and American English lies in the choice of
vocabulary. Some words mean different things in the two varieties for example:
Mean: (American English - angry, bad humored, British English - not generous, tight fisted)
Rubber: (American English - condom, British English - tool used to erase pencil markings)
There are many more examples (too many for me to list here). If there is a difference in usage,
your dictionary will note the different meanings in its definition of the term. Many vocabulary
items are also used in one form and not in the other. One of the best examples of this is the
terminology used for automobiles.
