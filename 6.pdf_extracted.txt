I. Introduction
Words are related to one another in a variety of ways. These relationships have words to
describe them that often end in the bound morpheme –nym
It may be more accurate to think of the lexicon as network, rather than a listing of words as in
a published dictionary.
Words are in different relations with each other, so they can be grouped in terms of their
relationship to each other. Synonymy, antonymy, polysemy, homonymy and hyponymy are
commonly accepted as the major possible relationships between words.

II. Synonymy
Synonymy refers the exact sameness or close similarity of meaning. Words that are close in
meaning are called synonyms.
Synonyms are traditionally defined as words different in sound and spelling but identical or
similar in meaning.
Why so many synonyms?
English is a language particularly rich in pairs of synonyms. The primary reason for this has to
do with the history of language and especially with the wholesome borrowing from other
languages..
Generally speaking, the native words are the simplest and most ordinary and tends to belong
to the ordinary colloquial language; The Greek or Latin borrowings are the most learned, most
formal and most technical and they tend to be used in the most formal context; French borrowings
stands between the native words and the Latin and Greek borrowings.
It is obvious that words are synonymous or similar in meaning only in a certain sense, not in
all their meanings.

Two types of synonymy:
‘strict’ or ‘absolute’ synonymy and ‘loose’ or ‘relative’ synonymy
 Strict synonyms refer to two words which are identical in meaning in all its aspects. They are
interchangeable in all contexts. The different choice would have no effect on the meaning,
style or connotation.
Strict synonyms are very rare, found only in words with a rather narrow range of senses. Thus
some linguists even argue that strict synonyms do not exist.
Strict synonyms is uneconomical; it creates unnecessary redundancy in a language.
 When we speak of synonymy, we mean ‘loose’ or ‘relative’ synonymy, where we find not
only a significant overlap in meaning between two words, but also some contexts where
they cannot be used interchangeably.
John found/discovered the basketball in the grass.
Maria Curie discovered radium in 1898.
*Maria Curie found radium in 1898.
Difference Among Synonyms
We often take the following things into consideration when we try to find the differences between
synonyms.
Different English dialects
Different stylistic meanings
Different connotative meanings
Different collocative meanings
III. Antonymy
Antonymy is a relationship of ‘meaning opposition’ that may hold between two words.
Antonyms can be defined as words which are opposite in meaning.
Major types of antonyms：
1) Gradable antonyms: gradable antonyms represent a more/less relation. The words can be the
end-points of a continuum（连续体）. Since they are gradable, they allow comparison.
2) Contradictory or complementary antonyms: These pairs are called contradictory antonyms
because they represent an either/or relation. The assertion of one implies the denial of the other.
They are non-gradable, so they do not allow comparison.
3) Converse antonyms: converse antonyms represent two-way contrasts. They are relational
antonyms (one presupposes the other).

IV. Polysemy
When a word is first coined, it is always the case that it has only one meaning (monosemic).
But in the course of development, the same symbol may be used to express new meanings. The
result is polysemy.
Polysemy shows the economy and efficiency of human languages.
There are two important processes in the development of meaning:
◙ Radiation（辐射式）
◙ Concatenation（连锁式）
V. Homonymy
Homonymy is the relation between two or more words that are spelled or pronounced in the
same way but differ in meaning. Such words are called homonyms.
Homonyms are considered different lexemes, primarily for the reason that they have
unrelated meanings and different origins.
There are several types of homonyms, in terms of the way of sameness:
◙ lie vi. / lie vi. — perfect homonyms
◙ tear n. / tear vt. — homographs
◙ coarse / course — homophones
Differentiation of homonyms from polysemous words:
1) etymology
2) semantic relatedness
VI. Hyponymy
Hyponymy is the sense relationship of inclusion that relates words hierarchically. The
underlying observation is that some words have a more general meaning, while others have a more
specific meaning, though referring to the same entity.
In semantics this relationship is described according to unilateral implication（单边/单向的
包蕴关系）.
Two special cases:
1) Taxonymy is the relationship in which “X is a kind of Y” (Cruse 1986). Not all hyponyms
are good taxonyms. Focal orientation; distinctive feature
2) Different from the kind-of relation displayed by hyponymy, meronymy involves
part-whole relation between words.
arm: body wheel: car
